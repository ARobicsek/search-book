3w • Edited • 

As someone who has debated the "should I go to residency?" question many times -- both internally, and with friends, mentors, and mentees -- I found Aditya Jain's article particularly interesting (and am grateful for his kind words about Counsel Health).

Ultimately, the answer has always been the same: it depends... it depends on who you are, what motivates you, and what you're solving for in your life.

But the article invokes a deeper question worth discussing: does AI make a physician's job more or less enjoyable?

The bull case is that AI uplevels physicians into supervisors of care rather than front-line delivery workers. For most people, supervision is more gratifying and less taxing, whether on the battlefield, the hospital wards, or in startup offices. Counsel as a platform for doctors to "approve" an AI's recommendation is not so different from an attending physician at a teaching hospital approving a resident's plan. As someone who has done both of these jobs, I can tell you they both are more enjoyable than practicing without leverage.

The bear case is that we've taken a job that used to be engaging, dynamic, and deeply purpose-driven and turned it into one of mouse clicks and keystrokes. But if we're being honest, I'd estimate most PCPs would describe their jobs this way even without AI.

It's also possible we're asking the wrong question entirely. Whether AI makes a physician's job more or less interesting is, at best, a secondary concern -- as much as I hate to admit it as a physician myself. 

The primary question should be: does AI make the patient's experience better? If AI delivers more accurate diagnoses and more consistent access at lower cost, then it should be celebrated. The concept of a physician is simply a human construct. The main goal is and has always been the service of the patient.
