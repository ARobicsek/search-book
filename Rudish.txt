Who should own AI in the C-suite? It’s a question posted by Harvard Business Review, and maybe one you’re having at your workplace.

While IT departments are likely leading the cause for many hospitals or healthcare systems, the truth is no single executive can — or should — own it.
AI cuts across nearly every domain of the enterprise from tech to operations, finance to compliance, talent and workforce to data governance.

Because AI systems increasingly influence decisions, workflows, and even autonomous “agentic” processes, the question isn’t “Who owns AI?” but “Who owns which AI-related decisions?”

The article argues that organizations should move beyond turf wars and instead design clear decision rights across the leadership team. AI governance becomes a coordination challenge, aligning strategy, data, risk, and operations, rather than assigning the technology to one department.

April is Stress Awareness Month. I have learned that managing stress rarely happens at my desk. As a leader, I also learned that managing stress isn't a one size fits all.

For me, the ultimate reset button is my family. Planning trips, exploring new places with my kids, attending their sports events and simply being present at home are the activities that keep me grounded and energized. I owe to my family to manage my stress and give them my best self.

As a leader, I remind my teams we have a responsibility to listen to teams and develop realistic solutions when our team members are stressed. A few things that come to mind can be as simple as asking team members what help them manage stress and, if possible, act on it. Or the solutions can be more complex like offering flexible scheduling that respects life outside work. In addition, EAP resources that are easy to use and resilience training that builds people up rather than placing the burden on them can go a long way especially if normalized!

Regardless, leading by example in stress management and wellbeing is the best strategy as teams will copy the behaviors they are witnessing!

The EU is taking a major step toward scaling AI in healthcare.

The European Commission has invited hospitals to join a new network of AI-Powered Advanced Screening Centers, focused on early detection of cancer and cardiovascular disease.

The initiative aims to move AI diagnostics from fragmented pilots into coordinated clinical deployment across all 27 EU member states. Participating hospitals will test AI tools in imaging workflows, validate their performance across diverse populations, and help establish shared standards for trustworthy clinical AI.

The broader goal is to build a federated health infrastructure that leverages European health data and imaging platforms to improve screening rates, reduce diagnostic disparities, and accelerate AI adoption in routine care.

If successful, this could become one of the largest real-world deployments of AI-assisted diagnostics globally.

I know I am interested in seeing how this comes together and what hospitals and health systems in the United States can learn from it.

Elizabeth Sellman, President and COO of Mount Sinai Downtown penned a piece for Becker’s Hospital Review on her experience of leading during disruption that touched me. Her reflection wasn’t wasn’t just about strategy or checking off the boxes during a major moment of change, but about showing up with compassion.

She reflects on leading through a hospital closure, an experience layered with uncertainty, grief, fear, and deep personal impact for staff and community members. What makes the story powerful isn’t just what was done operationally, but how it was done.

Instead of retreating into executive distance, she leaned in.
She showed up.
She listened.
She created space for emotion.
She answered hard questions with honesty.

In moments when people felt untethered, her presence created connection.
That’s what elevates this from a management story to a leadership story. 

Compassion wasn’t treated as a soft skill, it was the stabilizing force. By acknowledging the very real human experience unfolding around her, she strengthened trust, preserved dignity, and modeled resilience.

Disruption tests leadership. Human-centered leadership passes that test.

Unpopular opinion: “Why Should We Hire You?” is the wrong question to ask.

When I saw this headline in Forbes, that was my immediate reaction. While the point of the article is to help candidates navigate this type of borderline confrontational style of questioning (a good intention for sure), my main takeaway was on why this question is being asked in the first place.

When our talent team is evaluating candidates, we never ask this question. Instead, we work to get to the heart of where their interests and capabilities meet the client’s needs.

We may ask something like “Why are you interested in this opportunity?” And “What would you consider to be your area of expertise relevant to this position?
We want to make sure candidates can draw a connection between their experiences and what value they could add, not just how they meet the requirements.

It’s less about “why hire” and more about “where will you take the role?”

Have you heard of “experience intelligence”?

Experience Intelligence describes a strategic leadership capability rooted in designing and shaping experiences that people truly love, whether they are customers or employees. Leaders with this skill focus less on controlling outcomes and more on creating environments where people feel seen, capable, and proud.

It’s the core idea in this recent Harvard Business Review article about Disney’s new CEO, Josh D’Amaro. The writer, who has spent decades covering leaders, suggests that this quality is one of the things that allows “some leaders [to] consistently move others to action and why others fail even while holding power.”

He reflects on seeing D’Amaro interact with people who “responded because they felt seen, trusted, capable and proud of what they were a part of.”
It’s a really interesting take on a leadership quality that is fairly new to the lexicon. While it may be easy to see the value of “experience intelligence” for a company like Disney, the idea of deeply understanding and crafting the experience of your employees and customers could benefit other industries.

The increasing importance of patient experience in healthcare, for instance, requires leaders who make decisions about systems, processes and talent, with that mind. Preventing physician burnout, too, is perhaps more achievable by a leader with experience intelligence, who seeks to structure a work environment that makes people feel supported.

If you’re in healthcare or some other sector, what’s your take on “experience intelligence”?
